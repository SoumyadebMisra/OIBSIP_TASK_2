Alan Turing
1912-1954
"We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done."

Biography

Alan Turing was an English mathematician, logician, and computer scientist who lived from 1912 to 1954.

He is widely considered to be one of the fathers of computer science and artificial intelligence.

Turing attended King's College, Cambridge, where he studied mathematics and developed his interest in computing. He later worked at the Government Code and Cypher School during World War II, where he played a crucial role in breaking the German Enigma code, which helped the Allies win the war.

Turing continued his work in computer science and artificial intelligence. He developed the concept of a universal machine, which would become the theoretical basis for modern computing. He also proposed the Turing Test, a measure of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior equivalent to, or indistinguishable from, that of a human.

The Legacy

Alan Turing was a brilliant mathematician and logician who helped lay the theoretical foundations for modern computing, and his ideas and innovations continue to shape the field today.

Turing was also an important figure in the fight against fascism during World War II, using his skills to help break the German Enigma code and turn the tide of the war in favor of the Allies. His work in codebreaking helped save countless lives, and his legacy in this regard is still celebrated today.

Furthermore, Turing's tragic persecution for his homosexuality and his subsequent death have made him a symbol of the struggle for LGBTQ+ rights. His story serves as a reminder of the injustice and discrimination that marginalized groups have faced throughout history, and his courage and perseverance in the face of persecution have made him an inspiration to many.
